naturalnews.com  (WEB - en-US)

A site promoting unproven alternative medicine treatments and both medical and non-medical conspiracy theories, particularly the idea that vaccines are linked to autism.

Ownership and Financing

Natural News is owned by Mike Adams, described on the site as “an activist-turned-scientist” who claims to have cured himself of type 2 diabetes using natural remedies. Adams is also executive director of the nonprofit Consumer Wellness Center. Products including dietary supplements and so-called “superfoods” -- which are sold on the site -- are labeled as having been verified by Consumer Wellness Center. Adams also founded a separate site, Hoggwatch.com, promoting conspiracy theories about David Hogg, a survivor of the Parkland, Florida, school shooting. The site also earns revenue from advertising.  

Content

The site’s About page describes Natural News as a “science-based natural health advocacy organization” that is critical of the medical and pharmaceutical industries. Much of the content is aggregated from other sources, including scientific journals, laced with the personal opinions of the writing staff. These include opinions on health topics, with one writer calling flu shots the “greatest medical fraud in the history of the world.”

Health and nutrition topics are the main focus, with stories pushing “natural” remedies and attacking current health practices in overheated language. Water fluoridation, for example, is described as “one of the greatest crimes against humanity.” There are also stories treating purported supernatural phenomena, like demon possession, as accepted fact.

While Natural News has long promoted unproven or debunked theories such as the link between vaccines and autism and claims that the Zika virus originated with genetically engineered mosquitoes, it has branched out to write about non-medical conspiracy theories. Those include claims that there was more than one gunman in the October 2017 Las Vegas shooting and that a February 2018 dip in the stock market was the work of the “deep state” to remove Trump from office.

Credibility

Natural News has been repeatedly cited by scientific journals, fact-checking sites PolitiFact and Snopes, and other sources as a publisher of false and misleading information. Among its other false claims are: Cell phone usage can cause mental illness, the measles vaccine was the cause of the death for 108 children between 2005 and 2015, and the 2012 Aurora, Colorado, movie theater shootings were staged and the alleged shooter instructed to take the blame, “perhaps under hypnosis.”

Natural News has been aggressive in responding to claims that it pushes pseudoscience and false claims. For instance, it called PolitiFact “a propaganda arm of the Obama administration” and called liberal sites Slate, Salon, and Huffington Post “child killers” for doubting the site’s claims about vaccines causing adverse effects.

No corrections policy is posted on the site, although a contact form offers an option to suggest a correction.

Natural News did not respond to two messages using the site’s contact form asking about its history of promoting conspiracy theories and false claims, whether it has corrected errors in its reporting, how it separates writers’ opinions from facts, and its lack of sourcing.

Transparency

Mike Adams is the only content creator on the site with a biography. The names of other writers are included in their stories, but no biographical or contact information is provided. A sitewide contact form and phone number is provided for scheduling an interview with Adams. Advertisements are not clearly labeled, as stories often feature a link to what looks like another article from Adams but instead redirects to a product he sells.

Natural News did not respond to two emails using the site’s contact form asking about its blurring of advertising and content as well as its lack of information about writers other than Adams.

History

The site was launched in 2003.

Correction: An earlier version of this Nutrition Label gave the incorrect date of the mass shooting at a movie theater in Aurora, Colorado. The attack took place in 2012, not 2015.
