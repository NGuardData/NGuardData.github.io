Ownership and Financing

PRI.org is owned jointly by PRI/PRX, according to Alex Newman, the acting executive editor for digital with the organization. Public Radio International Inc. is a nonprofit based in Minneapolis.  PRX is based in Cambridge, Massachusetts. The two companies announced plans to merge in August 2018.

Both companies produce and distribute radio programs and podcasts, with a focus on serving public media broadcasters. Both earn revenue for distributing content and receive government grants and donations from foundations and individuals.

PRI.org solicits donations and runs advertising.

Content

PRI.org says on the site that its mission “is to serve audiences as a distinctive content source for information, insights and cultural experiences essential to living in our diverse, interconnected world.”

The site’s text and audio stories with international perspective on the news are often produced by producers of shows in different cities. Typical audio news reports on international news include stories about the 70th anniversary of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, peace talks to end the Yemeni civil war, and a Filipino fast-food restaurant opening in New York City. Many of these stories are featured on “The World,” a nightly radio show “that crosses borders and time zones to bring home the stories that matter”.

Other content includes extended audio clips and segments from different radio shows and podcasts from around the country, including shows such as “Studio 360” (“Public radio's smart and surprising guide to what's happening in pop culture and the arts,”), “The Takeaway,” (a daily news talk show co-hosted by Cook Political Report editor Amy Walter and former CNN reporter Tanzina Vega) and the podcast “LBJ’s War” (an examination of the president Lyndon Johnson’s decisions involving the Vietnam War).

Some content is supplied by other news organizations, such as Reuters, Agence France-Presse, and the Center for Public Integrity. PRI produces and shares resources with other international broadcasters such as the BBC in the United Kingdom and Canada’s CBC.

Credibility

PRI news stories are generally based on on-the-ground reporting, including interviews with witnesses and experts, public officials’ statements, and government documents. Segments from separate shows usually feature interviews with reporters, newsmakers, authors and subject experts. Headlines reflect the content of stories and interview segments. 

Between 2006 and 2009, PRI won three Alfred I. duPont-Columbia University Awards. One award was for a series exploring stem cell treatment. Another was for a series about the 2008 world financial crisis. The award was given for stories focused on a Muslim family living in post-9/11 New Jersey.

The site regularly publishes corrections, generally with italicized notes at the end of stories explaining the initial error. The website does not have a specific opinion section, but has labeled some first-person accounts and other columns as “Commentary” in small print above the story.  Newman told NewsGuard that that designation sometimes goes into headlines.

Transparency

PRI’s website generally lists an author for each stories and the radio show it is associated with. Author biographies are typically available, along with social media handles. The site identifies its board members and other editorial leaders. A Contact Us page provides PRI’s main postal address, telephone number and a form to communicate with different departments.

Public Radio International explains its nonprofit status on its About page. PRI.org provides details about finances and major donors in a Financials and Annual Report section. Logos for some donors, such as the Carnegie Corporation of New York, the MacArthur Foundation and the Ford Foundation, appear at the bottom of the website.

Funding and management details for PRX, the nonprofit content producer that is in the process of merging with PRI, are not posted on the website. Newman, the acting executive editor for digital for PRI, told NewsGuard, "As soon as the decisions about the future of the brands and the websites are made, we’ll start the work of updating the website.”

The site’s advertising is distinguished from editorial content.

History

PRI’s origins date to the founding in 1983 of American Public Radio, which was formed as a way to provide new programs to public broadcasters, according to its website. American Public Radio changed its name to Public Radio International in 1994. “PRI’s The World” launched two years later as public radio’s first global news show.
