aol.com  (WEB - en-US)

The website for internet service provider AOL, or America Online, publishing a global news section primarily of aggregated content. AOL was an internet pioneer in the 1990s and one of the best-known sites and brands in the U.S.

Ownership and Financing

AOL has been a subsidiary of the Verizon telecommunications company since 2015. In 2017, it became part of Oath Inc., Verizon’s new digital media division that includes new Verizon acquisitions Yahoo and The Huffington Post. The site earns revenue from advertising.

Content

News content is largely aggregated, with the original content provider’s logo and author listed. Topic sections include U.S. news, world news, politics, sports, science & technology, and entertainment.

Much of the content comes from the Oath-owned Huffington Post, along with Reuters and NBC News. Less frequently used contributors include Yahoo Sports (Yahoo is also part of Oath), AccuWeather, Business Insider, and Entertainment Tonight. Sponsored content appears on the right side and bottom of the home page under the heading “From Our Partners.”

Articles written by AOL’s own writing staff largely attribute reporting to other sources, including celebrity social media posts. Some articles in AOL’s lifestyle section feature exclusive interviews. Some stories are only credited to “AOL.com editors.”

Credibility

AOL has no posted corrections policy, but it has noted corrections made by other sites from which AOL takes its content. Two emails to AOL editors and Oath, and two phone messages left with Oath asking about AOL’s corrections policy, were not returned.

Opinion pieces are infrequently posted on AOL.com; its opinion section contains articles as old as 2010. Opinion pieces from AOL’s own writers are labeled as such. However, content posted from other sources is not always labeled. A Business Insider story that disclosed it was an opinion piece on its own site was missing a similar disclaimer when posted on AOL.com.

Transparency

No news editors are listed on AOL.com. The About page links to the main website of Oath, which includes no information on editorial policies for its individual brands. While writers of content posted from other sites are identified, no links or contact information for those reporters or AOL’s own writers are provided. Stories from other sources that deal with Verizon and posted on AOL don’t disclose that Verizon owns AOL. Two emails to AOL editors and Oath, and two phone messages left with Oath asking about its policy for writing about Verizon, were not returned.

History

AOL has gone through several ownership changes over its history, dating to the founding of Quantum Computer Services in 1985. Its name changed to America Online in 1991, where tens of millions of Americans became familiar with its dial-up browser and email service.

AOL fell from its spot as the dominant internet service provider in the late 1990s after merging with Time Warner. It was then spun off as an independent company and began ramping up its own news operations. In 2011, it acquired Huffington Post and scaled back its original content production.
