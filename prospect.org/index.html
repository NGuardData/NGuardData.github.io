Ownership and Financing

Run by nonprofit The American Prospect Inc., the magazine earns revenue from donations, grants, advertising, and subscriptions. According to the organization’s most recent Form 990 filed with the Internal Revenue Service, The American Prospect had approximately $1.7 million in revenue in 2016 and $1.8 million in expenses

Content

The American Prospect says on its About Us page that it aims “to advance liberal and progressive goals through reporting, analysis, and debate about today’s realities and tomorrow’s possibilities.”

The site provides commentary, analysis, and reporting on topics including trade, climate change, and politics. The summer 2018 edition of the magazine looked at the tax cut enacted in 2017 from a number of angles. Stories published in the issue and online included an analysis claiming that the tax reduction will not boost corporate investment and another arguing that the cut  “embodies the Republican culture of corruption.”

A section of the site called Trickle Downers houses articles the site says highlight “the folly, dysfunctions, and sheer idiocy of feed-the-rich economic policies.”

The site links to its podcast, Provocations, hosted by The American Prospect co-founder and co-editor Robert Kuttner. The American Prospect also publishes several email newsletters.

Credibility

The American Prospect regularly links to top news sites such as The Atlantic, RealClearPolitics.com, and the Washington Post. It also cites credible sources outside the news media, including campaign finance data from the nonprofit Center for Responsive Politics and information from the U.S. Government Accountability Office. Writers also regularly conduct their own interviews and research. Headlines accurately reflect content.

The site’s liberal perspective, which is clearly disclosed, permeates its reporting and analysis, but articles are typically fact-based and well-sourced -- which is why NewsGuard has determined that it responsibly handles the difference between news and opinion.

For example, an November 2018 analysis of public transportation in the Sun Belt, written by a Northeastern University professor, examined transportation policies in Houston, Salt Lake City, and Nashville, including historical data. The article concluded that expansion of transit “depends largely on the vagaries of political leadership, timing, and luck.” 

In August 2017, the magazine made news after then-White House chief strategist Steve Bannon called The American Prospect co-editor Kuttner and discussed infighting within the administration. Kuttner’s article about their conversation included Bannon’s statement that some of his White House rivals were “wetting themselves.”  Bannon left his White House position a few days later. In an article about Bannon’s departure, USA Today said The American Prospect article was “apparently the last straw.”

The website publishes corrections, typically in italics at the bottom of articles.

Transparency

Articles generally include the name of the writer, with brief biographies and links to their Twitter profile. A Masthead page identifies The American Prospect’s top editors, often providing email addresses. The Masthead also lists the Prospect’s writers, contributing editors, and members of its board of directors.

Advertisements are distinguished from editorial content.

An About Us page indicates the publication is operated as a nonprofit, but The American Prospect does not identify major donors. NewsGuard has therefore determined that The American Prospect does not meet the standard for disclosing information about its ownership and financing.

NewsGuard sent an email to The American Prospect’s media inquiry email address and left a phone message inquiring about the donor-disclosure issue, but did not receive a response.

History

The American Prospect was founded in 1990 by Kuttner and Paul Starr, the magazine’s co-editors, and Robert Reich, who would later serve as President Bill Clinton’s labor secretary. In a 1989 prospectus for the magazine, linked on the site’s About Us page, the founders wrote that they hoped The American Prospect would contribute to a resurgence of liberalism, drawing parallels to the boom in U.S. conservative thought over the prior two decades. “American liberalism badly needs the same kinds of focused sponsorship and organization,” they wrote.

Former staff members for The American Prospect include Vox.com co-founders Ezra Klein and Matthew Yglesias, New York Magazine writer Jonathan Chait, and Talking Points Memo editor and publisher Josh Marshall.
