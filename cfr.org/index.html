Ownership and Financing

The Council on Foreign Relations (CFR) is a nonprofit, nonpartisan membership organization open to U.S. citizens who apply for membership. Revenues come primarily from returns on endowment investments, individual and corporate member dues, and Foreign Affairs magazine. Revenue for the fiscal year ending in June 2016 (the latest IRS filing that is publicly available) was $83.8 million. The site states that it does not receive money from the U.S. or foreign governments.

Content

CFR describes itself as a resource intended to help those who formulate policy and other interested citizens “better understand the world and the foreign policy choices facing the United States and other countries.” CFR.org’s Mission Statement notes that the CFR “takes no institutional positions on matters of policy,” and carries out its goals by convening meetings and debates, sponsoring task forces to produce reports, and “providing up-to-date information and analysis about world events and American foreign policy on its website, CFR.org.”

CFR.org content includes interviews with global leaders, analyses of pressing international issues, and international relations-themed quizzes. The majority of articles are blog posts written by CFR fellows. Blog posts have included “Nord Stream 2: Is Germany ‘Captive’ to Russian Energy?,” “Trump Is the First President to Get Turkey Right,” “Women Candidates Face Harassment and Threats of Violence in Zimbabwe,” and “The Internet Was Created in the United States, But China Will Shape Its Future.” In January 2019, the website ran “Agonizing Over Afghanistan,” CFR President Richard Haass’ essay arguing that peace negotiations will not succeed, and that the government and its U.S. and NATO allies cannot win militarily. The article originally ran in Project Syndicate, which publishes commentary on global affairs. Other CFR analysts who write for the site include Max Boot and Sebastian Mallaby.

Credibility

Articles on CFR.org are supported by responsibly credited sources. Headlines accurately represent content, which is generally written by experts known in their fields. For example a 2018 blog post, “Did Russia Really Dump Its U.S. Debt?,” examined media speculation that the Russian government sold U.S. debt to protect itself from U.S. government economic influence. The article showed that Russia likely only sold 45% of its Treasury holdings rather than the 87% reported by outlets including CNN. CFR demonstrated this by quoting four external websites and incorporating raw data from sources including Russia’s central bank and the Belgian bank Euroclear. The post is written by two professional geo-economists.

The site publishes corrections, typically in the form of a note at the bottom of the affected article identifying what change has been made.

Transparency

Articles on CFR.org consistently name their authors and feature a link supplying biographical information and contact details for him or her. The site also features pages naming the organization’s leadership, board of trustees, and CFR staff, as well as pages detailing CFR’s funding and identifying its donors.

History

The CFR was founded in 1921 to “afford a continuous conference on international questions affecting the United States, by bringing together experts on statecraft, finance, industry, education, and science.” Via its publication Foreign Affairs the CFA has hosted numerous famous contributors, with authors including political scientist-author Francis Fukuyama, former French President Jacques Chirac, Hillary Clinton, and actor George Clooney. The outlet has also produced articles that became historically influential. In 1947 U.S. diplomat George F. Kennan wrote, under the pseudonym “X,” “The Sources of Soviet Conduct,” in which he argued, “It is clear that the main element of any United States policy toward the Soviet Union must be that of long-term, patient but firm and vigilant containment of Russian expansive tendencies.” This suggestion would later grow into the U.S. policy of “containment” of communist influence around the world. In 1993 Samuel P. Huntington wrote an influential Foreign Affairs article, “The Clash of Civilizations?,” in which he argued that future wars would be fought between cultures rather than countries.
