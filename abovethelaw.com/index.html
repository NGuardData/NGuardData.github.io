Ownership and Financing

AbovetheLaw.com is owned by Breaking Media, a privately held company that owns six other industry-focused publications, including Dealbreaker.com for finance news, Fashtionista.com for fashion, and BreakingGov.com for government professionals. Breaking Media also produces e-newsletters, events, and social media channels for what it describes as “affluent business communities.”

Breaking Media generates revenue through advertisements on its sites, as well as research and website services, mobile products, and event marketing.

Content

AboveTheLaw.com, which describes itself as “a behind-the-scenes look at the world of law,” offers news and commentary ranging from gossipy columns and industry scoops to legal analysis. It is aimed mainly at legal professionals. The site provides a significant amount of service journalism, including career advice with features such as “It’s Not All About The Billables: Making Productive Use Of Downtime” and “When It’s Time To Let Your Associate Counsels Shine.” AboveTheLaw.com publishes about 20 stories a day.

The main navigation on the site includes eight sections, starting with Big Law and Small Law (about large and small law firms.) Other sections focus on law schools, in-house lawyers, and legal technology. A Career Center section provides data on law firm salaries, firm rankings, and job listings. The site hosts a podcast, called Thinking Like A Lawyer.

Credibility

In 2012, AboveTheLaw.com was described by Business Insider as the law industry’s “favorite insider publication.” The site has four full-time editors who alternate between writing news and commentary, Elie Mystal, the site’s executive editor, told NewsGuard in a phone interview. The bulk of the site’s contributors provide commentary, he said, noting that everyone who writes full-time for Above The Law has a law degree.

While opinionated articles are sometimes labeled as columns, Above The Law does not have a consistent method for differentiating news from opinion. Abovethelaw.com’s About page describes the site as a source for “news and insights,” but those categories are not delineated for readers. For this reason, NewsGuard has determined that Above the Law does not responsibly handle the difference between news and opinion.

 “I wouldn’t call most of what we do news,” Mystal said. “I don’t think we try to sell ourselves as doing anything other than offering our spin on the news.”

The site frequently runs corrections, appended to the article that included the error, although it does not state a corrections policy. “I won’t stealthily delete the wrong thing and rewrite the post as if nothing happened,” Mystal said. “I’ll do a strikethrough of the wrong information and then within the post we’ll post the correction.” In response to NewsGuard’s question about the site’s lack of a stated corrections policy or an easy means for readers to propose a correction, Mystal said, “While I think it’s obvious that if you have an issue you should email us, that probably needs to be stated more forthrightly somewhere on the page.”

When it believes it has missed the mark, Above The Law has made an effort to hold itself accountable. For example, after federal appeals judge Alex Kozinski retired in 2017 amid a spate of sexual-harassment complaints, Above the Law came under fire from readers for its positive coverage of Kozinski over the years. An Above the Law editor, Kathryn Rubino, responded to the criticism in a post. “When Above the Law — or any website that provides commentary along with the news — reports on a story, we come at them with our own biases,” she wrote. “Frankly, those takes are a part of the job and what keeps readers coming back, so it isn’t a problem per se that [editor David] Lat’s personal affection for Kozinski made its way into his coverage of the man. But we need to be aware and ever vigilant that the way our stories are framed do not do a disservice to the legal community, and as more information becomes available, updating and honing those takes.”

The site publishes a range of perspectives. David Lat, the founding editor of the site -- now an editor at large -- was vice president of the conservative Federalist Society chapter at Yale Law School. He continues to speak at Federalist Society events and has taken conservative positions in his Above The Law columns. Mystal, who took over as executive editor after Lat stepped down in 2017, is an outspoken liberal, often appearing as a commentator on MSNBC. An article he wrote in September 2018 about Supreme Court nominee Brett Kavanaugh was titled “All Of Your Pro-Kavanaugh Vote Arguments Are Trash.” On the same day, the site ran a column by Lat headlined “The ‘Pro-Kavanaugh’ Case For Hearings Into The Latest Accusations.”

Transparency

Articles include the name of the writer, and clicking on the name leads to a page where the user can see a list of that writer’s recent stories. Some of these author pages include biographies and contact information, and others do not, but the articles themselves include bios for the writers at the bottom. Contact information for AboveTheLaw.com’s editorial staff is listed on its About page, and includes Mystal and other site editors.

The site also provides a generic form through which users can submit tips about “leads, gossip, leaked documents, embarrassing photos, or anything else juicy.” AboveTheLaw.com discloses that is owned by Breaking Media and provides contact information for its owner, as well as a link to Breaking Media’s website.

AboveTheLaw.com runs some features that do not identify the writer. The LawProfBlawg, for example, is written by “an anonymous professor at a top 100 law school,” according to the site. Another series is authored by “Earl Grey,” whom the site says is a “first-year student of color…” Mystal told NewsGuard that they would not be able to write their columns without pseudonyms. Mystal said all of the site’s writers are known to him and have been vetted.

Because the site does not disclose names, contact details, or biographical information about some its regular columnists, NewsGuard has given the site a “No” in the category for disclosing information about content creators.

Above The Law hosts a ”legal innovation center” called Evolve the Law, which is described as an online “membership community for all stakeholders interested in shaping the future of law.” Members get access to events, legal directories, and a private Slack group of “thought leaders,” among other resources.

When NewsGuard asked if there are any ethical concerns around the fact that some of the people about whom the site writes are paying a premium for special access, Mystal said: “I don’t know who’s buying in. Nobody’s telling me. And I’m not asking.”

The site provides detailed explanations of the methodologies it uses in determining its law school and law firm rankings. Advertising and sponsored content on Abovethelaw.com are clearly labeled. 

History

Breaking Media and AboveTheLaw.com were founded in 2006. Above The Law began publishing rankings in 2013, and bought Evolve the Law in 2018. Prior to becoming a journalist, Mystal earned a J.D. from Harvard Law School and worked as a litigator at the firm Debevoise & Plimpton.
