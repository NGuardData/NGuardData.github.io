Ownership and Financing

Metro is owned by DMG Media, a subsidiary of the Daily Mail and General Trust (DMGT). In addition to Metro, DMG Media publishes the Daily Mail and its website, MailOnline. The Daily Mail and General Trust also owns companies including events firm DMG Events, risk modeling company Risk Management Solutions, and a 49 percent stake in Euromoney Institutional Investor PLC, which publishes financial magazines. The Daily Mail and Metro’s offices are in the same building in Kensington, London.

Daily Mail and General Trust is a publicly traded company. However, the company has been largely controlled by the Harmsworth family of newspaper publishers since Alfred and Harold Harmsworth founded the Daily Mail in 1896. Harold Harmsworth, then-chairman of the trust,  was given the aristocratic title of “the 1st Viscount Rothermere” in 1919. All subsequent chairmen have had the same hereditary title, including Harold Harmsworth’s great grandson and current chairman, the 4th Viscount Rothermere (often referred to in the UK as “Lord Rothermere”).

The site runs advertisements and sponsored content.

Content

Metro’s editor Ted Young described the newspaper in an October 2015 interview with The Guardian as “a news aggregator for people going into work. They don’t want long spiels. They want information and they want it to be even-handed.” Distributed for free and predominantly at commuter stations, Metro is written to consist of quick reads which -- unlike many other British newspapers -- can appeal to all potential readers regardless of their political affiliation.

Research by Metro suggests the average age of its readers is 37 -- 22 years younger than the average for British weekday newspapers generally. The paper’s print edition is the highest-circulation newspaper in the country.

Prominent content sections on metro.co.uk include News, Sport, Entertainment, Soaps, Lifestyle, Platform, Video, and Platform (an opinion section). Typical headlines on the website range from straight news, such as “There will be no People’s Vote as Theresa May condemns call for second Brexit referendum” and “Met Office issues weather warning for snow and ice as Arctic blast hits UK,” to more typical tabloid headlines, such as “Millionaire jailed for leaving girlfriend to bleed to death after ‘rough sex’,” “Thomas Markle begs daughter Meghan to get in touch with him after months of silence,” and “Thirsty Thursday gets Christmas off to a very merry start.”

Credibility

Metro is regulated by the UK’s Independent Press Standards Organisation (IPSO), through which publications contractually agree to be held liable for breaching IPSO’s Editors’ Code of Practice. The code includes standards such as accuracy, privacy, harassment, and discrimination. A section of metro.co.uk that provides guidelines for contributors instructs them to comply with IPSO’s Code of Practice.

Press Gazette, a website focused on the United Kingdom’s news industry, reported in March 2017 that Metro employs approximately 60 editorial staff members. The newspaper publishes some original stories, and frequently bases accounts on television reports and videos shared on social media. Some exclusive reports on metro.co.uk include the articles, “Boris Johnson given 24 hours to respond or face legal action over Brexit ‘lies’” (December 2018), “Kevin Hart stepping down from Oscars is ‘positive evolution’, explains The Upside director” (December 2018), and “Boris Johnson misses deadline and will now face legal action over Brexit claims” (December 2018).

The Economist described Metro in March 2018 as “a studiously apolitical commuter freesheet.” Unusual among most British national newspapers, Metro avoids endorsing any party or position. During the run-up to the referendum on “Brexit,” or the U.K.’s European Union membership in 2016, the Leave campaign approached Metro seeking to take out a front-and-back page advertisement worth £250,000. The request was only granted on the condition that the Remain campaign also take out a similar advertisement for another £250,000. The Remain cover ad ran the day after Leave’s ad.

During the 2015 U.K. general election, Metro ran an interview with every major party leader except the United Kingdom Independence Party’s Nigel Farage, who refused an interview request because of what Farage perceived as a bias against Farage’s UK Independence Party.

Metro.co.uk has a dedicated opinion section that hosts its columns and analysis pieces. The newspaper is generally balanced when reporting on politics.

Metro.co.uk regularly publishes corrections and has a Corrections and Clarifications page. Corrections are generally published in italics at the bottom of the relevant article.

Transparency

Articles on metro.co.uk consistently provide their authors’ names, but do not generally disclose any other information. Names of Metro editors are not posted on the website in a manner accessible to average users. A link in Metro’s “Privacy & Cookies Policy” page leads to the DMG Media site, which lists the company’s editors and corporate leaders.

Metro is identified in small print at the bottom of the website as a property of Associated Newspapers Limited, which does not meet NewsGuard’s standard for disclosing ownership in a user-friendly manner.

Advertisements and sponsored content on the website are clearly distinguished from editorial content.

The Metro’s digital editor, Deborah Arthurs, did not respond to two NewsGuard emails asking about the site’s disclosure of information about content creators, editorial leadership, and ownership.

History

Jonathan Harmsworth was inspired to found the British version of Metro after noticing commuters in Stockholm reading a free newspaper by the same name during a trip to Sweden, according to a Daily Mail and General Trust history of the newspaper. Metro launched in London in March 1999, four years after Sweden’s Metro paper began publishing.

Metro was not the U.K.’s first free daily newspaper -- that title went to the Birmingham Daily News, which launched in 1984. However, it was the first of a series of free newspapers in London, including City A.M. and eventually the Evening Standard. By 2011, Metro was publishing in 50 cities around the U.K. It launched its website in July 2001.
