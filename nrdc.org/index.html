Ownership and Financing

The Natural Resources Defense Council, based in New York, is financed by donations, government and foundation grants, and endowment funds. The NRDC reported $177.1 million in total revenue for the 2016-17 fiscal year, according to financial reports posted on the website.

The site does not run advertisements.

Content

The website’s editorial content is primarily divided into two sections -- Our Experts and Our Stories.

The experts section, generally written by NRDC staff members, reports on actions taken by the organization. For example, the post “31 Groups Urge Gov Cuomo to Double Down on Energy Efficiency” discusses a letter sent by the NRDC and other organizations to New York Gov. Andrew Cuomo. Other posts in the experts section cover environmental topics, such as California’s carbon emissions, a proposed mine in Alaska, and smog in India.

Posts in the Our Stories section typically consist of in-depth reporting by contributing writers. These posts are usually part of onEarth, a digital magazine that says it is editorially independent of the NRDC. Stories cover politics, climate change, and animal conservation. An About page for onEarth says its editors make the publication’s editorial decisions, and that NRDC donors or advocates do not have prior review of its stories.

Pages on a third section, called Our Work, outline the council’s positions and work on issues that include climate resilience and energy.

Credibility

The Natural Resources Defense Council prominently displays its mission at the top of its About Us page, saying the organization “works to safeguard the earth—its people, its plants and animals, and the natural systems on which all life depends.” This perspective is evident throughout the website. The About page for onEarth says, “Our journalism has a point of view” and adds that its journalists “believe solutions exist to the problems affecting our health and our environment.”

Many posts quote experts or cite academic research, including the council’s research. Articles from onEarth regularly feature original reporting. Others link to reporting done by credible news outlets, such as The New York Times and Politico. The website’s writing is straightforward and based on facts and quotes cited in the text.

The site’s headlines are generally opinionated but represent the articles’ content, including the October 2018 headlines “Tarantulas the Size of Your Head? Yeah, We Need to Save Them, Too” and “Freeing Towns From the Tyranny of the Automobile: A How-To Guide.”

The About page for onEarth says, “Any errors in fact are promptly corrected.” However, because NewsGuard could only locate one correction notice published on the website in the past year, it has determined that the website does not regularly correct or clarify errors. The NRDC did not respond to a NewsGuard telephone call and email seeking comment about the website’s corrections practices.

Transparency

A Contact Us page provides email addresses for general inquiries, as well as telephone numbers for NRDC offices.

Stories and blog posts on the site typically identify their author by name. Readers can click on an author’s name to access a page with the author’s photograph and brief biography. Photographs used on the site have proper attribution.

Pages on NRDC.org name and describe the professional affiliations of the members of the organization’s board of trustees and its leadership team.

A Financials page links to a recent IRS form and audited financial statement for the NRDC, noting that it is a nonprofit. However, the website does not share information about the council’s major donors, which does not meet NewsGuard’s standard for disclosing information about ownership and relevant financing. The NRDC did not reply to a NewsGuard telephone call and email seeking comment about why the site does not provide this information.

The site does not run advertisements.

History

The NRDC was founded in New York in 1970 by law students and lawyers led by John Adams, a former U.S. prosecutor and Wall Street attorney. Adams wanted to create an organization for the environmental movement that resembled the American Civil Liberties Union and the NAACP Legal Defense Fund. The organization’s early victories included securing the removal of lead from gasoline, after the NRDC sued the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency in 1972. In 1994, the NRDC and other plaintiffs settled a lawsuit with the EPA that led to the ban of 36 cancer-causing pesticides found in processed food.

In 1983, onEarth, then known as the Amicus Journal, won a George Polk Award for special interest reporting.
