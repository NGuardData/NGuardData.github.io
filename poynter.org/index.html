Ownership and Financing

The website is owned by the Poynter Institute for Media Studies, a nonprofit institute based in St. Petersburg, Florida, that offers courses for working journalists and journalism students. Poynter also owns the Tampa Bay Times newspaper, fact-checking website PolitiFact, and the e-learning site News University.

Poynter generated close to $6 million in revenue in 2016, according to its latest tax filings. It is funded by tuition, donations, and contributions from corporations and foundations.

Content

Poynter.org publishes news, analysis, and commentary on the practice of journalism and the press. Content is geared toward "journalists and citizens who value journalism," the site states. Articles cover topics including journalism innovations, news literacy, and efforts to combat the spread of misinformation. A section called Leadership highlights the work of leading journalists and Poynter's own leadership training program. The In Case You Missed It section features aggregated news articles about the media.  

Poynter authors often write about the ethical issues journalists confront. For example, the site has published articles about how journalists should handle social-media content during a mass shooting, the New York Times' decision to publish an op-ed critical of President Trump by an anonymous author claiming to be a senior member of his administration, and the circumstances under which a journalist can ethically break a promise to keep information off the record.

The site features articles by Poynter’s International Fact-Checking Network, a three-person team that monitors fact-checking efforts worldwide. The group writes about fact-checking trends and best practices, including coverage of Facebook’s efforts to fight misinformation and the growth of fake and satirical fact-checking sites.

Credibility

Poynter.org articles are typically balanced and well-sourced. Headlines accurately reflect content.

Poynter runs corrections, appending them to articles and explaining the mistake and how it was corrected. Its code of ethics, posted on the site, includes a corrections policy, which states that the site "provides timely responses, clear corrections and prominent acknowledgments that a mistake was made and addressed."  

Opinion pieces are labeled as commentary.

Poynter occasionally reviews ethics guidelines from other news organizations, and provides advice to news organizations that seek it. The Washington Post, for example, contacted the Poynter Institute before it decided to post disturbing photos taken at Abu Ghraib prison during the Iraq war.

Poynter's International Fact-Checking Network has developed a code of principles promoting nonpartisan, transparent fact-checking practices. News teams around the world can apply to become signatories of the code of principles if they meet its standards. More than 50 fact-checking organizations are signatories, including The Conversation Australia, the Washington Post's Fact-Checker, and Agence France Press's AFP Factuel. Facebook also requires its fact-checking partners to get approved by the International Fact-Checking Network.

In 2013, Craig Silverman, who at the time worked at Poynter, won a Mirror Award from the Newhouse School at Syracuse University for Best Commentary.

Transparency

The site's About page provides a detailed description of the Institute and links to pages about its mission, team, history, and work in the community.

Poynter's code of ethics includes a link to a list of funders who donated more than $50,000 to the Institute, which includes the Charles Koch Foundation, the Democracy Fund, and the John S. and James L. Knight Foundation.

The Our Team page presents short biographies of staff and faculty members, along with their social media handles. It also links to profile pages with contact details. A Contact page lists general phone numbers and email addresses to send feedback or questions.

Advertisements are labeled. The site also publishes branded articles with the label "Paid Content."

History

The Poynter Institute, originally named the Modern Media Institute, was founded in 1975 by the owner of the St. Petersburg Times, Nelson Poynter. The nonprofit's trustees changed its name to the Poynter Institute in 1984.

The website was launched in 1999.
