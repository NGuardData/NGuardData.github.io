Ownership and Financing

Al Jazeera English (AJE) is part of the Al Jazeera Media Network, a media conglomerate encompassing Al Jazeera Arabic (AJA), the online youth-targeted news outlet AJ+, and several other Al Jazeera foreign language affiliates. Al Jazeera was founded in 1996 by Qatar’s then-ruling Emir, Sheikh Hamad bin Khalifa Al Thani. Originally named “Al Jazeera Satellite Network” and run as a public organization by the Qatari government, Al Jazeera changed its name and legal status in 2006 to become what the site describes as a “private corporation established for the public benefit.” That year, the network launched Al Jazeera English (AJE).

Al Jazeera continues to be owned and funded by the Qatari government. Juan Carlos Van Meek, Al Jazeera’s Director of Digital Innovation & Programmes, told NewsGuard that the amount of funding the network receives from the Qatari government "varies every year" and that "more of our revenue is coming in from advertising  and sponsorships.”

Sheikh Hamad bin Thamer Al Thani, a former official with Qatar's Ministry of Information and Culture, is chairman of the Board of Directors of Al Jazeera, which oversees all of the channels under the Al Jazeera umbrella.

The site runs advertisements.

Content

AJE runs globally focused news coverage and investigations mixed with analysis and commentary. Headlines in September 2018 included “Syria's war: Iran's Zarif calls for Idlib to be 'cleaned out',” “Mediterranean refugee numbers drop but crossing is deadlier: UN,” “Punishing tax stops refugees studying at Hungarian universities,” and “Interracial marriages on the rise in China.”

Al Jazeera showcases a wide range of viewpoints. The organization first came to many westerners’ attention in 2001 when it began broadcasting recordings made by al-Qaida leader Osama bin Laden. The Guardian wrote in 2017 that in Arab countries, the launch of Al Jazeera had allowed “all kinds of previously banned voices to be heard, from the Israelis and Muammar Gaddafi to Chechen rebels, the Taliban and Osama bin Laden.” Broadcasting content that is taboo elsewhere has prompted criticism of the network, some of it based on misconceptions. For example, former U.S. Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld inaccurately claimed in 2005 that Al Jazeera had broadcast footage of beheadings.

Al Jazeera English is distinct from Al Jazeera Arabic (Aljazeera.net). While the two often share bureaus, they function as two separate outlets. Foreign Policy magazine wrote in February 2014 that “The two channels are entirely separate: different management, different editorial lines, even physically separate buildings in Doha.” AJE’s coverage is sometimes considered biased in the West, but it is more restrained that the overtly opinionated AJA. Hussein Ibish of the Arab Gulf States Institute told The Economist in July 2017 that Al Jazeera Arabic was like “Fox News on steroids; it goes much further, flirting with the promotion of violence.”

Credibility

The Al Jazeera Media Network maintains over 65 bureaus globally and produces a significant amount of original reporting. News articles appearing on aljazeera.com are generally supported with a range of attributed sources and photographs. Headlines accurately represent content.

The site regularly runs corrections. When a news story is corrected, a note is added at the top of the article describing what was incorrect and how it has been remedied.

Al Jazeera has prominently reported on several historically significant events. The organization was the only news agency in Iraq during the first weeks of Operation Desert Fox in 1998 and again in Afghanistan during the 2001 US-led invasion. During the Arab Spring, Al Jazeera became the leading source for information about the turmoil: Business Insider reported in January 2011 that the outlet’s web traffic increased by 2,500 percent during the Egyptian revolution. The Atlantic lauded Al Jazeera in January 2012, proclaiming “Al Jazeera correspondents are the first on the scene, and Al Jazeera anchors and interviewers provide the most detailed follow-up, discussion and analysis of breaking events in the Arab world.”

Al Jazeera has been accused of biased reporting and popularizing extremist content in line with the strategic goals of the Qatari government. In particular, critics often allege that Al Jazeera promotes violent Islamism, anti-semitism, and anti-Americanism, and that it harshly covers governments at odds with Qatar — but such criticism is most often made of Al Jazeera Arabic.

A number of countries have restricted, censored, or outright banned Al Jazeera for alleged misreporting. During the 2017-2018 blockade of Qatar by neighboring states including Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates, Bahrain, and Egypt, one of 13 demands made of Qatar was that it shut down Al Jazeera. Israel later joined in this demand and revoked press passes for Al Jazeera journalists in the country.

Although individual news stories on AJE rarely editorialize, the site’s coverage of Qatar’s rivals consistently skews towards unflattering stories while its comparatively rare reporting on itself is almost always positive. Headlines from September 2018, for example, include “'This shouldn't be happening': Civilian deaths spike in Yemen,” “Saudi accused of impeding UN-backed war crimes probe in Yemen,” and “Saudi human rights activists awarded 'alternative Nobel prize’” - the latter focusing on Saudi activists given a prize for protesting the Saudi government. An otherwise-positive sounding article, “Saudi-UAE alliance to open humanitarian corridors in Yemen,” ends by noting at least 10,000 people have been killed in Yemen since “the Saudi-Emirati-led coalition” intervened in 2015. By contrast, September 2018 headlines about Qatar included “Qatar first Gulf nation to grant permanent residency to expats,” “Qatar lifts exit permit system for migrant workers,” and “UAE used Israeli spyware 'to target Qatari emir, Saudi prince.’”

This, coupled with persistent allegations that Qatar’s government leans on Al Jazeera’s leadership to push Qatar’s geopolitical agenda, significantly damages AJE’s reliability in covering such stories. In NewsGuard’s opinion, what accurate reporting AJE carries out does not make up for the breach of credibility caused by its propagandistic approach to covering Qatar and its neighbors. For these reasons, NewsGuard has concluded that AJE fails to gather and publish news responsibly and fails to distinguish between news from opinion.

Transparency

News articles on aljazeera.com rarely name their authors, with stories often attributed simply to “Al Jazeera and News Agencies.” Opinion articles are credited to their authors. The site does not feature a central staff list of AJE’s writers and editors or provide contact or biographical information about them.

In February 2019, Al Jazeera stated that it was in the process of developing a central directory for its content creators.

The site’s About Us page states that Al Jazeera is “funded in part by the Qatari government.” The page also features a “Leadership at Al Jazeera” tab, which lists the network’s Chairman of the Board and Director General, as well as its Managing Directors and Executive Directors. This includes the directors of Al Jazeera Arabic and Al Jazeera English, and the Director of News. The page links to biographies for each individual.

Advertisements are clearly labeled.

History

Al Jazeera was founded in 1996 in a bid to use Qatar’s growing oil and gas wealth to boost the small, historically vulnerable country’s diplomatic and cultural power abroad. Many of the organization’s founding journalists had previously worked at an Arabic BBC channel which operated in Saudi Arabia until censorship by the Saudi government forced the station’s closure. In 2013, Al Jazeera launched Al Jazeera America, a cable and satellite television channel targeted at the U.S. The channel closed in 2016 in the face of persistently low viewership.

Editor’s Note: This label was drafted by Bron Maher, whose review rated AJE green for “gathering and publishing news responsibly” and “distinguishing news from opinion.”  Maher argued that, regardless of its ownership by a government, AJE is not unique or exceptional among news outlets for clandestinely skewing its coverage to promote a self-interested agenda. While not opposed to being critical of such outlets, Maher advocated that rating Al Jazeera red in these criteria demands holding privately owned publications to the same standard. The co-CEOs and editors-in-chief disagreed, maintaining that a site surreptitiously owned by a government that funds and publishes the site to advance the government’s interests cannot in any respect be considered to be publishing news and information responsibly or separating news from opinion.

Editor’s Note: In February 2019, following changes made to AlJazeera.com’s About Us page, which disclosed the role of the Qatar government, NewsGuard determined that the site now meets its standard for disclosing information about ownership and financing, as well as about its leadership. NewsGuard has changed its ratings from red to green for these criteria.
