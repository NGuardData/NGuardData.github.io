Ownership and Financing

Truthout is a 501(c)3 nonprofit organization. According to its About Us page, 80 percent of the site’s and organization’s annual budget for the past five years has come from individual donations. The organization also receives grants from foundations including the Craigslist Charitable Fund, The Annie E. Casey Foundation, and the Park Foundation. Truthout states in its Editorial Independence Policy that it does not accept advertising or corporate backing.

The site’s editor-in-chief is Maya Schenwar, a journalist who has written extensively about prisons. The President of Truthout’s Board of Directors is Robert Naiman, a policy analyst and activist who writes on the Middle East. Naiman helped organize the 2011 campaign that sought to break Israel’s blockade of Gaza.

Content

The publication describes its mission as working “to spark action by revealing systemic injustice and providing a platform for transformative ideas, through in-depth investigative reporting and critical analysis.” Truthout publishes reporting, analysis, and opinion -- often blended together.

The site’s sections include Economy & Labor, Media Justice, Prisons & Policing, and Human Rights. The Series section features investigative projects, in-depth analysis, and commentary on themes including “Bringing Down the New Jim Crow,” “Gaza on Fire,” and “The Rise of the American Corporate Security State.” The site also produces cartoons, podcasts, and videos.

Truthout.org often runs stories critical of governments and corporations. Examples of articles in the site include “Equality and Territory: The Common Struggle of Indigenous Women in the Andes;” “Fossil Fuels Are Next in Line for a Bailout – Here’s How We Respond;” and “The Trump White House Is in Even More Disarray Than We Feared.” Notable truthout contributors have included economist and New York Times columnist Paul Krugman, BuzzFeed investigative journalist Jason Leopold, and linguist and social critic Noam Chomsky.

Truthout.org often runs attributed content from liberal organizations and other news sites, including DemocracyNow.org, Propublica.org, and Salon.com. Typical articles included, “No More Compromises: We Need Immigration Amnesty Now;” “On 10th Anniversary of Crash, Warren Says: Break Up the Banks, Jail the Bankers;” and “Trump’s Incoherent Trade Policy Is Crony Capitalism.”

Credibility

Truthout regularly publishes original, exclusive reporting. In September 2018 the site revealed that Virginia prisons were enacting a policy banning visitors from using tampons or feminine hygiene cups. The policy was dropped following public outcry the day after Truthout’s story was published.

The site has reported extensively on fracking. In 2013, Truthout reporter Mike Ludwig, using documents obtained through a Freedom of Information Act request, showed that fracking was being used on offshore oil rigs in the ecologically sensitive Santa Barbara Channel. Subsequently, legal settlements between environmental groups and the U.S. Interior Department led to a moratorium on new permits for offshore fracking in California.

In 2016, Truthout broke a story showing that the Obama administration had approved more than 1,500 applications for offshore drilling permits, including permits for fracking in the Gulf of Mexico. The outlet revealed that regulators had issued over 300 “categorical exclusions” exempting some drilling plans from environmental review, and that some of the 1,500 permit applications had been approved at the height of the Deepwater Horizon disaster.

Articles on the site are marked “news,” “analysis,” or opinion, to indicate the degree to which the writer’s point of view is included.

Truthout discloses its political viewpoint. On its About Truthout page, the site describes its mission as “revealing systemic injustice and providing a platform for progressive and transformative ideas.” The page links to a post that details Truthout’s “editorial approach,” noting that the website is against “mass incarceration, white supremacy, widening economic inequality, and a militarized form of global capitalism that disproportionately affects people of color and women worldwide.”

Truthout’s Submission Guidelines state that articles featured on the site must name and contextualize sources, cite from “reliable” third parties, and include external links to substantiate assertions. Truthout.org regularly publishes corrections and features a stated corrections policy on its About page.

In 2018, Truthout staff reporter Dahr Jamail was awarded the “Izzy Award” by the Park Center for Independent Media for his “outstanding achievement in independent media,” reporting on climate change.

Transparency

Articles on Truthout include the names of the author and biographical information, but not contact details. Truthout Community Liaison Joseph Peterson told NewsGuard that the organization “respects the privacy” of its authors “and does not publish contact details unless instructed to do so by the author directly.” 

The site features a staff list and names its contributors, editors, board of advisors members, and board of trustee members. Truthout posts its annual financial reports going back to 2011. The reports name donors who contributed over $500 in that year and organizations that donated to the organization.

Truthout’s About section describes the site’s Editorial Independence Policy, stating that “Readers and foundations who donate to Truthout do so in their knowledge that our editorial principles and judgment are in no way influenced by those donations.”

History

According to Crunchbase, Truthout was founded in 2001. Its sister site, news aggregator BuzzFlash, was founded in 2000.
